Post-Op: Recovery

Recovery is individual. Most people go home in 2–4 days, feel steadier by week 2, and rebuild strength over the next few months.

What the first 6 weeks usually look like

  • Hospital (days 1–4): walking the day of/after surgery, pain control, urine and labs watched closely.
  • Week 1–2: expect fatigue. Short walks daily. No tub baths, pools, or hot tubs until incisions fully closed.
  • Weeks 3–6: walking > 30 minutes most days; light chores. Many return to desk work by 2–4 weeks; physical jobs can take 4–8+ weeks with clearance.

Medications & labs

  • Take anti-rejection meds exactly on time. Set alarms; do not double up if you miss—call your team.
  • Lab schedule: very frequent at first (often 2×/week), then weekly, then less often as things stabilize.
  • Before-dose labs: if your team asks for a “trough,” have blood drawn before your morning dose.

Incision, pain, and activity

  • Keep the incision clean/dry; pat dry after showers. Watch for spreading redness, drainage, or odor.
  • Use stool softeners with narcotics; transition to acetaminophen if approved. Avoid NSAIDs unless your team says otherwise.
  • No lifting > 10–15 lbs and avoid core-straining moves until cleared. Drive only when off narcotics and you can brake/turn safely.

Food, fluids, and sleep

  • Hydrate steadily unless told otherwise. Aim for pale yellow urine.
  • Balanced meals; limit salt to help blood pressure. If food tastes “off,” small frequent meals are fine.
  • Some anti-rejection meds interact with certain citrus (e.g., grapefruit). Ask your pharmacist what to avoid.

When to call

  • Call your team now: fever > 101°F, vomiting that prevents meds, new/worsening swelling at the incision, urine output dropping, new leg swelling or calf pain.
  • Call 911 / go to ER: severe chest pain, trouble breathing, severe bleeding, confusion, inability to wake/stay awake.

Questions to ask: What’s my activity limit and for how long? When can I return to work or driving? Which symptoms should trigger an urgent call vs. a message?

This page is educational, not medical advice. Follow your team’s specific instructions.
